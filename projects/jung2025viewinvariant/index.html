Towards Video Large Language Models for View-Invariant Video Understanding

Minjoon Jung, Junbin Xiao*, Junghyun Kim, Il-Jae Kwon, Byoung-Tak Zhang, Angela Yao
Seoul National University, National University of Singapore
*Corresponding Author
Paper Code

Abstract

Most video large language models (Video-LLMs) are focused on exocentric (third-person) videos, leaving their generalization to egocentric (first-person) views underexplored. This raises a question: Can Video-LLMs achieve consistent temporal comprehension across videos showing the same content from different viewpoints? To answer this question, we introduce EgoExo-Con (consistency), a benchmark of synchronized exo-ego video pairs with human-refined queries. It covers two temporal understanding tasks, temporal verification and grounding, and checks for correct and consistent answers across different viewpoints. Our benchmarking results show that Video-LLMs struggle to provide consistent answers across viewpoints, though specific performance varies across domains, lengths, and tasks. Furthermore, even trained with paired videos of both viewpoints, they often fail to yield consistent temporal comprehension or even underperform models trained on a single view. For improvements, we propose a scalable and efficient method that iteratively mines samples where the model shows consistent comprehension across viewpoints and encodes them in parallel to generate responses conditioned on both viewpoints. This allows the model to progressively learn consistent temporal comprehension, yielding performance comparable to state-of-the-art models.

Overview

Consider a model that correctly answers ``yes'' to the question ``Is the person holding a sandwich?'' when presented with an exocentric video. This demonstrates the model can recognize the action ``holding a sandwich.'' However, if shown the corresponding egocentric video of the same scene, does the model still answer ``yes''? i.e., is the learned knowledge for identifying actions and objects in specific video moments preserved well enough to yield consistent responses across viewpoints? To study this, we focus on the temporal aspects in videos through two different tasks: temporal verification, and temporal grounding. Specifically, we query a model with the same question across synchronized video pairs and check for consistent responses. The above example measures binary consistency, evaluating whether the model provides ``Yes.'' for both videos.

Dataset

We collect and filter synchronized video pairs along with their queries. Then, we use GPT-4o to refine the queries by addressing elements that cannot be reliably inferred from both viewpoints. Human evaluation and refinement follow to validate queries.
